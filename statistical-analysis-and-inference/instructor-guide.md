# Module 11 — Statistical Analysis and Inference: Instructor Guide

**Module Time:** 120 minutes

---

## What This Module is About

This module introduces learners to statistical analysis through correlation and regression, focusing on interpreting relationships between variables and communicating data-driven conclusions clearly and responsibly.

Learners move from descriptive exploration to formal statistical reasoning, learning how to quantify relationships and explain their meaning in context.

---

## Core Objectives

- Perform correlation analysis to assess relationships between variables  
- Conduct simple regression analysis using Python statistical libraries  
- Interpret coefficients, direction, and strength of relationships  
- Understand significance and limitations of statistical results  
- Communicate findings clearly in non-technical language  

---

## Must-Hit Topics

- Difference between exploration and statistical inference  
- Correlation: meaning, strength, direction, and limitations  
- Regression as an explanatory tool  
- Interpreting coefficients and model outputs  
- Communicating results responsibly and clearly  

---

## 1. From Exploration to Statistical Questions  
**(Time: ~20 minutes)**

**Purpose:**  
Help learners transition from descriptive exploration (Module 10) to formal statistical questioning.

**Talking Points:**

- Review what EDA can and cannot answer  
- Moving from “patterns” to “relationships”  
- Framing analytical questions suitable for correlation or regression  
- Importance of validated, analysis-ready data  

**Teaching Tips:**

- Ask learners to propose questions based on trends seen in Module 10  
- Reinforce that statistics answer *specific* questions, not everything  
- Emphasise that poor questions lead to misleading analysis  

---

## 2. Correlation Analysis: Meaning and Misuse  
**(Time: ~30 minutes)**

**Purpose:**  
Introduce correlation as a way to quantify relationships while highlighting its limitations.

**Talking Points:**

- What correlation measures (and what it does not)  
- Direction vs strength of relationships  
- Pearson correlation and basic assumptions  
- Correlation matrices for multiple variables  
- Common pitfalls: causation, spurious correlations  

**Teaching Tips:**

- Use visual examples (scatter plots) alongside coefficients  
- Repeatedly stress “correlation ≠ causation”  
- Encourage learners to interpret *magnitude*, not just sign  

---

## 3. Introduction to Regression Analysis  
**(Time: ~30 minutes)**

**Purpose:**  
Provide a conceptual and practical introduction to regression as an explanatory tool.

**Talking Points:**

- Difference between correlation and regression  
- Dependent vs independent variables  
- Interpreting regression coefficients conceptually  
- Understanding model outputs at a high level  
- Regression as explanation, not prediction (at this stage)  

**Teaching Tips:**

- Keep math light; focus on interpretation  
- Use plain-language explanations for coefficients  
- Encourage learners to explain results verbally before writing them  

---

## 4. Interpretation, Communication, and AI Reflection  
**(Time: ~18 minutes)**

**Purpose:**  
Develop analytical judgement and communication skills around statistical results.

**Talking Points:**

- Translating statistical outputs into non-technical language  
- Role of visualisation in supporting interpretation  
- Risks of over-interpreting statistical results  
- Using AI tools to support (not replace) reasoning  

**Teaching Tips:**

- Have learners critique example interpretations (good vs bad)  
- Emphasise that uncertainty and limitations should be stated clearly  
- Encourage critical evaluation of AI-generated explanations  

---

## Wrap-Up Reflection  
**(Time: ~2 minutes)**

**Key Takeaways:**

- Correlation and regression help quantify relationships, not prove causation  
- Interpretation and context matter more than numerical output  
- Clear documentation and communication are essential for credibility  

**Next Steps:**  
Learners move on to the lab, where they perform correlation and regression analysis on a validated dataset, visualise relationships, and communicate findings in clear, non-technical language.
